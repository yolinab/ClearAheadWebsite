Before you build anything,
read the science.

Your hypothesis is unvalidated. That's not fatal — it means your first job is research, not product. Here's exactly how to do it as a student with no budget, starting from zero.

1
Your hypothesis chain — what needs to be true

Your product rests on a chain of claims. Each link needs scientific support. If any link breaks, your prediction model fails. This is what you're actually going to the literature to validate or disprove.

Claim Status What to look for
Context switching between cognitively different tasks has a measurable cost ✓ Supported Well-established in cognitive psychology. Gloria Mark (UC Irvine) has 20 years of workplace interruption research. Start here.
Meeting density correlates with experienced cognitive fatigue ~ Partial Meeting fatigue is real (Microsoft EEG study, 2021). Whether density specifically predicts load is less clear. Look for meeting science literature.
Calendar structure (visible in advance) reflects future cognitive demand ? Gap This is your key missing link. Calendar as a predictive proxy for workload has almost no direct research. You may be the first to study this specifically.
Fragmentation of deep work blocks impairs cognitive performance that day ~ Partial Attention residue research (Sophie Leroy) supports this. Not yet directly linked to calendar visibility.
People can act on a morning prediction to meaningfully improve their day ? Gap No direct research on this. Closest: implementation intentions literature (Peter Gollwitzer). Behavioural change from predictions is your product's real bet.
The prediction is accurate enough to be useful without biometric data ? Gap This is your largest unknown. Sleep alone accounts for a huge variance in cognitive performance. Calendar-only may be too noisy without body data.
Honest framing The first two claims have solid scientific backing. The last four are where the gap lives. Your research job is not to find proof that you're right — it's to map exactly where the evidence ends and your hypothesis begins. That boundary is your actual product's scientific foundation.
2
Where to get papers — free, as a student

You have more access than you think. Use these in order — try the top ones before resorting to the grey options.

Google Scholar Free
Start every search here. Many papers have a direct PDF link on the right side. If not, click "All versions" — a free preprint often exists. Also shows who cited a paper, which is how you trace the field forward.
Semantic Scholar Free
AI-powered academic search. Better than Google Scholar for finding related papers and understanding a field quickly. Shows "highly influential citations" which saves you enormous time.
Your university library portal Uni login
Log in through your university's library portal (usually via Athens or Shibboleth). This unlocks Elsevier, Springer, Wiley, JSTOR, and more. Use this before anything else for paywalled papers — it's probably broader than you realise.
PubMed Free
Best for neuroscience, sleep, HRV, and biomedical research. Free abstracts always, full text often via PubMed Central. Critical for the body-signal side of your hypothesis.
PsyArXiv Free
Preprint server for psychology. Papers posted before or alongside journal publication — free, often the same quality as published versions. Critical for cognitive and organisational psychology.
ResearchGate Free
Authors post their own papers here. If the PDF isn't there, you can request it directly from the author — most researchers respond within days and are genuinely happy someone is reading their work.
arXiv Free
Preprints for computer science, AI, and applied ML. Useful when you get to the technical side — sensor-based cognition, passive inference, wearable signal processing.
Email the author directly Free
Find the corresponding author's email in the paper abstract. Write: "I'm a student researching [topic], I'd love to read your paper on [X], could you share a PDF?" Response rate is 60–80%. Researchers are flattered, not annoyed.
Tip: the citation chain trick Find one good paper, then do two things: (1) look at its reference list — those are the foundational papers you need. (2) In Google Scholar, click "Cited by N" — those are the papers that built on it, showing you where the field went next. You can map an entire research area in a few hours this way.

4
How to read a paper — efficiently, not exhaustively

Most people read papers wrong — cover to cover, getting lost in methods. The right approach is fast and targeted. A paper should take you 15–30 minutes unless it's directly central to your core claim.

01
Abstract first (2 min)
Does this paper actually answer one of your six hypothesis questions? If no — stop. Don't read further. Move on.
02
Conclusion before Methods (5 min)
Jump to the Discussion or Conclusion. What did they actually find? What are the limitations they admit? This tells you whether the paper supports your chain.
03
Figures and tables (5 min)
Most empirical findings are in the figures. Read the captions. You can often understand the key finding without reading a word of methods.
04
Introduction for context (5 min)
The intro tells you what other papers matter. It's a curated reading list. Highlight every paper they cite that sounds directly relevant to you.
05
Methods only if critical (10 min)
Only read methods if you need to understand how they measured something, or if you want to replicate or build on their approach.
06
Note your verdict (2 min)
Write one sentence: what does this paper say about my hypothesis? Supports / contradicts / irrelevant / partially relevant. You'll thank yourself later.
Your reading log format Keep a simple spreadsheet: paper title | authors | year | which hypothesis claim it addresses | what it found | supports / contradicts / neutral | one quote worth keeping. Do this for every paper. After 20 papers you'll have a literature map of your hypothesis.
5
What you're building — a literature map

The end goal of this research phase is not a reading list. It's a clear answer to: which parts of my hypothesis are scientifically supported, which are extrapolations, and which are genuinely novel? That answer is your product's scientific framing — and eventually, its defensibility.

If you find this… It means… Your move
Strong evidence that calendar fragmentation predicts cognitive performance Your core hypothesis is pre-validated. Someone has likely tried to commercialise this. Find out why they failed or didn't. Build on the finding, cite it prominently, reach out to the researchers directly.
Evidence for individual components (switching cost, meeting fatigue) but no integrated study The gap is real and scientific. Your product is the applied integration of disconnected findings. This is the most common outcome. Your scientific claim is "we synthesise X, Y and Z findings into a practical prediction." Be explicit that it's extrapolation.
Research suggesting individual differences are so large that population-level predictions are unreliable A generic model doesn't work. Personalisation and a feedback loop aren't features — they're requirements for the product to function. Pivot the product to focus on personal calibration from day one. Self-tuning model, not fixed weights.
Research showing sleep / HRV dominates cognitive performance variance Calendar-only prediction is insufficient. The signal-to-noise ratio without biometrics may be too low to be useful. Biometric integration moves from "nice to have" to "required for accuracy." Build the integration argument into the product pitch.
Almost no research at the intersection of calendar structure and cognition You have a genuine gap to fill — but also no validation. You're doing novel research, not product development. Design your MVP as a research instrument first. Publish the findings. This builds credibility that no competitor can buy.
6
Your action plan — four weeks to a literature foundation

You don't need to read everything. You need to read enough to know what the field has and hasn't established. Four focused weeks is sufficient for a literature foundation.

Week 1 — Core cognitive psychology
Understand attention, switching costs, and workplace fragmentation
  • Set up your university library access — log in, confirm which journals you can access
  • Search Google Scholar for "Gloria Mark attention fragmentation" — read her 2004 CHI paper and her 2008 paper on cost of interrupted work
  • Search "Sophie Leroy attention residue" — read her 2009 paper on why it's so hard to focus on task B when you haven't finished task A
  • Search "context switching cognitive cost" on Semantic Scholar — find 3 papers, read using the 6-step method above
  • Start your reading log spreadsheet
Week 2 — Meeting science and workday structure
Understand what research exists on meeting load specifically
  • Search "Steven Rogelberg meeting science" — he's the world's leading meeting researcher, find his review papers
  • Find the Microsoft Research 2021 paper "The effects of remote work on collaboration" — it includes EEG meeting fatigue data
  • Search "meeting overload knowledge workers" on Google Scholar — read 3–4 papers, note their definitions of overload
  • Search "calendar fragmentation productivity" — this is your direct gap. Note what you do NOT find — absence of literature is data
  • Look for any paper that uses calendar data as a proxy for cognitive load. If you find one, it's your most important paper.
Week 3 — Sleep, HRV, and biometric predictors
Understand how well body signals predict next-day cognitive performance
  • Search PubMed for "HRV cognitive performance next day" — this is the scientific basis for Oura's readiness score
  • Search "sleep quality working memory" — you'll find extensive research. Note the effect sizes (how much variance does sleep explain?)
  • Search "ultradian rhythm cognitive performance" — Kleitman's basic rest-activity cycle (BRAC) is the scientific basis for the 90-min focus window idea
  • Read the abstract of Matthew Walker's lab papers on sleep deprivation and executive function (search "Walker sleep prefrontal cortex")
  • Note: when you find that sleep explains 40%+ of cognitive variance, write that down — it's the strongest argument for biometric integration
Week 4 — Synthesis and gap mapping
Build your literature map and write your scientific framing
  • Review your reading log — which hypothesis claims are supported, partial, or unvalidated?
  • Write a one-page "scientific foundation" document: what the literature supports, what it doesn't, and where your product sits
  • Search for 2–3 papers on "ecological momentary assessment" — this is the methodology you'll use if you want to run your own small study
  • Search "implementation intentions if-then planning" (Gollwitzer) — this is the science behind whether morning predictions change behaviour
  • Identify 2 researchers whose work is most central — consider emailing them. "I'm a student building a product based partly on your research on X" is a legitimate and often welcomed email.
What you'll have after 4 weeks A reading log of ~25–35 papers. A clear map of which claims are validated, partially supported, or novel. A one-page scientific framing you can show investors, researchers, or potential users. And most importantly — you'll know whether your hypothesis is worth building on, before you've built anything.
7
Key researchers — people doing relevant work

Following these researchers on Google Scholar (click "Follow" on their profile) means you'll see new papers as they're published. Several are also active on Twitter/X.

Gloria Mark
UC Irvine. 20+ years studying workplace interruptions, attention fragmentation, and digital distraction. The foundational researcher for your context switching claim.
Steven Rogelberg
UNC Charlotte. World's leading meeting scientist. His work on meeting load, meeting recovery, and the science of better meetings is directly relevant.
Sophie Leroy
University of Washington. Attention residue — why switching tasks before finishing leaves cognitive traces that impair subsequent performance. Critical for your switching cost claim.
Matthew Walker
UC Berkeley. Sleep science. His research on sleep deprivation and prefrontal cortex impairment establishes why sleep is the strongest single predictor of next-day cognitive performance.
Peter Gollwitzer
NYU. Implementation intentions — "if-then" planning. The science of whether forming a specific plan (like a morning briefing recommendation) actually changes behaviour. Directly relevant to your product's action layer.
Roy Baumeister
Ego depletion research — the idea that willpower and cognitive resources are finite and deplete across a day. Controversial (replication issues) but conceptually important. Know the debate.
